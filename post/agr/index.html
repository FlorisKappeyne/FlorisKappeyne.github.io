AGR: The Fastest Drifter

April 14, 2017

The project

AGR: The Fastest Drifter is an Anti-Gravity Racing game, made with the custom cross-platform (Windows and PS4) Voodoo Engine. Created by a team of 6 programmers at first (the first block of 8 weeks), and 19 people (7 programmers, 12 designers and artists) at the end, it is one of the biggest projects I have worked on so far. In the end, it featured a fully functioning racing game for both single player and local multiplayer, audio, particle effects, several post processing effects on top of the physically based rendering, a complete game-flow with UI and intro cinematics and a suite of tools usable by designers to tweak the game while playing it.

What I did

In the beginning of the project, I set up our asset pipeline. Its main feature was a standalone tool that converted OBJ and FBX files into our own binary format, for fast in engine loading. Later in the project, this was replaced by a Maya plugin that did the same thing.

Later in the first block, I started on UI support and made our game prettier, so that artists and designers would choose our game for next block.

The first week of the second block was for prototyping a game. I worked on a grappling hook prototype in unity, which was fun to play but not feasible in 3D (the prototype was mainly 2D), or at least not in 8 weeks.

Later in the second block, my work shifted to the UI of the game. I worked together with a designer (Jordi Engels) and constantly set up and iterated upon our UI. I programmed most of the game-flow starting with the intro cinematics (the logo of our school, and then a small video that a designer with too much free time made), the main menu, level and car selection screens, and then the main game with its HUD, pause screen, and victory screens. Jordi could tweak the position, image, size, rotation, text and other related variables of all UI elements on screen through an ImGui menu. The UI worked with both controller and keyboard, and was in the end fully complete and functional.

As a small side note, the intro cinematic wasn’t actually a video. There was no audio, and each frame was presented as a separate texture I loaded from disk, which was a terrible hack but it looked fancy (as long as you loaded from an SSD, not a network drive). But hey, it worked!
